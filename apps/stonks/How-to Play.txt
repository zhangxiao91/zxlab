The game is a 30-day stock-market tactics run. Each day lasts about five minutes and represents one trading day. The player controls a fund with cash, holdings, influence, reputation, and regulatory heat. The objective is to grow net worth by trading fictional listed companies while managing liquidity, timing, crowd behavior, rival whales, quants, news, and regulator attention.

The market contains a fixed set of fictional stocks across sectors such as tech, biotech, property, consumer, resources, finance, defense, and energy. Each stock has a price, daily price limit, market cap, float, liquidity, sentiment, attention, shareholder composition, average holder cost, quant presence, institutional presence, and heat. Small caps are easier to move but harder to exit. Large caps are harder to manipulate but safer to hold. Some stocks are speculative story stocks; others are defensive blue chips or distressed names.

The defining mechanic is the daily limit-up and limit-down system. A normal stock can only rise or fall by a fixed percentage in one day. When a stock reaches its upper limit, it cannot rise further. Extra demand becomes a buy queue. If the buy queue is strong, the stock is “sealed” at limit-up. If selling pressure increases and the queue weakens, the board may break, causing panic. Much of the game revolves around creating, defending, exploiting, or breaking these limit-up boards.

Each trading day begins with pre-market news. News can affect the whole market, a sector, or a specific stock. It changes sentiment, attention, credibility, fear, greed, and heat, rather than directly forcing prices. A policy rumor may increase attention in tech; a financial warning may weaken property stocks; a clarification may destroy a prior rumor. The player reads the news, estimates where attention will flow, and chooses whether to accumulate, attack, defend, short, or wait.

During the opening auction, the player sees expected openings, active news, sector sentiment, possible whale activity, and early order pressure. This phase lets the player prepare before the intraday simulation begins. A player might quietly accumulate a low-float stock before retail notices, sell a position before bad news hits, or prepare to attack a rival’s fragile board.

Intraday trading is the main game. The market updates every second by default, with 300 ticks per trading day. Prices move according to aggregate buy and sell pressure from the player, retail herd, rival whales, quants, institutions, news, and noise. The player can market buy, split buy, market sell, hidden sell, defend a limit-up board, pull support, push a rumor, spread fear, commission a report, or buy a bear contract. These actions are simple, but their consequences depend heavily on timing, visibility, liquidity, and crowd psychology.

The player is constrained by T+1 inventory. Shares bought today cannot be sold until the next trading day. This makes commitment meaningful. If the player buys aggressively to push a stock into limit-up, they may be trapped overnight. If the board breaks before they can exit, they may become the bagholder. This prevents instant pump-and-dump play and makes exits as important as entries.

Retail traders are simulated as one aggregate herd per stock. The herd is composed of momentum traders, dip buyers, news followers, gamblers, bagholders, and panic sellers. Their composition changes as the stock moves. A strong sealed limit-up board attracts momentum traders and gamblers. A weak board increases panic sellers. A broken board can trigger mass selling. Retail does not behave rationally; it amplifies belief, fear, and visible price action.

Quants are mostly invisible. Each stock has a quant presence value that affects how sharply it responds to momentum, overextension, weak boards, news surprise, and visible player activity. Quants may strengthen a clean trend, but they are especially dangerous when a board becomes weak. If the player creates an obvious, fragile pump, quants may attack it and cause the board to break. The UI should only show estimates such as “fast money suspected” or “algorithmic selling rising,” not exact quant actions.

Whales are individually simulated major actors. They have cash, positions, styles, risk tolerance, heat tolerance, preferred sectors, and intentions. A Pump Lord tries to accumulate small caps and drive them to limit-up. A Quant Knife attacks crowded weak boards. A Value Wall sells into overextended rallies. A Rescue Whale supports large caps during market weakness. A Bagholder Whale creates fake support to attract exit liquidity. The player has to infer whether whales are helping, competing, dumping, or baiting.

Liquidity is dynamic. A stock with high attention may have more volume but also more volatility. A sealed limit-up stock may show a huge buy queue but have little real sell liquidity. A panic stock may have endless sellers and no buyers. This means paper profit is not always real profit. The player can be rich on screen but unable to exit without collapsing the price.

The player also has influence actions. Pushing a rumor can raise attention and retail greed, but it increases heat and may later trigger a clarification. Spreading fear can break a rival board or help a bear contract, but it is risky and suspicious. Commissioning a report is slower and less explosive, but safer. Influence actions should change how the market reacts; they should not guarantee a price move.

Heat is the regulator system. Each stock has heat, and the player’s account has heat. Heat rises when trading is too visible, a stock has repeated abnormal moves, the player uses rumors, the player pulls support, or the player appears to pump and exit. At low heat, nothing happens. At medium heat, warnings and clarification risk increase. At high heat, the stock may receive an inquiry, halt, or become restricted. At extreme account heat, the player may face investigation or run-ending penalties.

The closing auction is the final pressure point of each day. Weak boards may break near close. Whales may defend one last time, dump into demand, or attack a fragile stock. Closing price becomes the next day’s reference price, so ending the day sealed, broken, or limit-down has major consequences for tomorrow.

After the close, the game settles the day. Locked shares become sellable for the next day. News decays or expires. Heat may decay or trigger regulator events. Whale intentions update. Bear contracts move toward expiry. Stock sentiment and attention adjust based on the day’s events. The player sees updated cash, net worth, realized profit, unrealized profit, heat, and portfolio risk before advancing.

The player wins by compounding capital over 30 days, but the final evaluation should distinguish between liquid profit and fragile paper wealth. A player who ends with huge unrealized gains in illiquid limit-up stocks is not as successful as one who actually exited. The final screen should show net worth, realized profit, unrealized profit, max heat, halted stocks, best trade, worst trade, and final grade.

The intended experience is practical and tense: find where attention will go, enter before the crowd, use enough force to move the market, let others carry the trade, exit before belief collapses, and avoid leaving obvious fingerprints. The market should feel alive because every major move changes the behavior of retail, whales, quants, institutions, and regulators.
